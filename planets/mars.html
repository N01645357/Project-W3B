Overview

Mars remains a focal point for scientific study and potential human exploration. It continues to captivate our interest due to its similarities to Earth and the possibility of harboring clues about the evolution of rocky planets and potential life beyond Earth.

Notable Features

Olympus Mons: The largest volcano in the solar system, towering about 13.6 miles (22 kilometers) high and spanning approximately 370 miles (600 kilometers) in diameter.

Valles Marineris: A vast canyon system stretching roughly 2,500 miles (4,000 kilometers) long, up to 7 miles (11 kilometers) deep, and in some places about 120 miles (200 kilometers) wide, making it one of the largest canyons in the solar system.

Exploration

Rovers: Several rovers have been sent to Mars, including Sojourner, Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance. These robotic explorers have provided valuable data about the planet's geology, climate, and potential for habitability.

Landers and Orbiters: Numerous landers and orbiters, such as Viking 1 and 2, Phoenix, InSight, MAVEN, and the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, have studied the planet's surface, atmosphere, and geological features.

Moons

Phobos: Larger of the two moons, Phobos, is irregularly shaped and relatively close to Mars. It orbits the planet at a very low altitude, completing an orbit in less than eight hours.

Deimos: Smaller and more distant, Deimos, is also irregularly shaped. It takes about 30 hours to complete an orbit around Mars.
